Welcome

Most scientific disciplines borrow mathematics. Our group creates it. The Algebraic Systems Biology group develops new mathematical theory — in algebra, topology, and geometry — and then does something rare: makes it directly applicable to open problems in biology and medicine.

There are very few groups in the world doing both of these things at once, at this level. This is one of them.

Creating mathematics for biology

New mathematics, built for the complexity of living systems

Biology does not fit neatly into the mathematical frameworks that were built for physics or engineering. Living systems are nonlinear, high-dimensional, and irreducibly messy. Their data does not live in flat Euclidean space — it lives on curved surfaces, in tensor structures, in topological shapes that standard statistical tools cannot even see, let alone analyse.

Applied Algebraic Geometry & Tensors

Biological data is not two-dimensional. Algebraic geometry gives us the tools to work in the higher-dimensional spaces where the real structure lives — and to know, with mathematical certainty, when that structure can be uniquely recovered.

  • Details

Topological Data Analysis

Shape is information. Topology lets us detect and measure geometric structure in biological data — across scales, across noise — capturing features that algebra and statistics alone cannot see.

  • Details

Software & Computational Mathematics

New mathematics requires new algorithms. The group builds the computational tools that make its theoretical methods usable — by other researchers, in other systems, at scale.

  • Details

Bridging precision and complexity

Where algebraic precision meets biological uncertainty

Biological data is not clean. It is incomplete, noisy, high-dimensional, and shaped by prior knowledge that is itself uncertain. The mathematical frameworks in this layer are designed to operate in that reality — combining the rigour of algebra and geometry with the messiness of real data, the uncertainty of statistical inference, and the structural knowledge encoded in biological networks.

Identifiability, Statistics & AI

Before asking what a model predicts, we ask a harder question: can its parameters ever be uniquely determined from data at all? If the answer is no, no amount of computation will produce a trustworthy result.

  • Details

Dynamical Systems & Mathematical Modelling

Living systems are not static. They evolve, oscillate, bifurcate, and self-organise — and understanding why requires mathematics that can describe change, stability, and the sudden transitions between them.

  • Details

Biological Networks

Every cell is governed by networks — of genes, proteins, signals, and feedback loops. This prior knowledge about how a system is wired is too valuable to ignore, and too complex to reason about without algebra and combinatorics.

  • Details

Morphology & Spatial Biology

Where a cell is matters as much as what it is. Geometric and topological methods reveal spatial organisation in tissues and imaging data that no standard analysis would find — including cell types and structures that were previously invisible.

  • Details

Translational applications

Answering biomedical questions that could not be answered otherwise

The word translational has a precise meaning in biomedical science: research that moves between mathematical or experimental insight and clinical or therapeutic application. The group's work is translational in exactly this sense — not as an aspiration, but as a track record.

Omics

A single modern experiment can describe the activity of every gene, every protein, in a tissue sample. The challenge is not producing this data. It is finding the mathematical framework that can reveal what it actually means.

  • Details

Oncology

Cancer does not follow a single script. Tumour cells rewire their signalling in ways that vary between patients, between cell populations, and over time — and understanding that variation requires mathematics that can find interpretable structure in high-dimensional, heterogeneous data.

  • Details

Immunology

The immune system is one of the most complex dynamical systems in biology — capable of extraordinary precision and catastrophic dysregulation, often governed by differences too subtle for any single experiment to isolate. Mathematical modelling makes it possible to ask questions that experiment alone cannot reach.

  • Details

Neuroscience

Neural circuits encode information in geometry as much as in activity. Topological and network methods developed by the group reveal hidden structure in how the brain represents the world — structure that only becomes visible when you have the mathematics to look for it.
